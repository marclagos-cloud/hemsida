A plain-language guide

What is ABA?

If you've just started exploring support for your autistic child, you'll run into the term ABA quickly, often with strong opinions attached. This page is my attempt to explain it simply: what it is, what it looks like in practice, and honest answers to the questions parents ask me most.

What does ABA mean?

ABA stands for applied behavior analysis. At its heart, it's the science of how people learn: how new skills grow, and how the world around us can make things easier or harder.

In practice, it means looking closely at what helps your child. What makes communication easier? When does play come naturally? What turns an everyday routine into a struggle, and what makes it work? ABA is a structured way of answering those questions and using the answers, in small steps, at your child's pace.

ABA is not one fixed method. It's a set of tools, and it can look very different from one practitioner to another. That's why it's worth asking any practitioner how they work, not just what it's called.

What does a session actually look like?

Mostly, it looks like play, because it is play. We might build, draw, chase bubbles or line up trains, whatever your child already loves. That's where children feel safe, and where learning happens naturally.

Inside that play, I work on the things we've chosen together as a family: perhaps asking for help, making a choice, taking turns, or trying a new word or sign. I follow your child's lead, keep track of what actually helps, and adjust as we go. Sessions usually happen at home or in school, in your child's everyday world, and you'll always know what we're working on and why.

I've read that ABA is controversial. Is it?

Some of it, historically, deserves the criticism. You may come across strong words about ABA online, often from autistic adults whose experiences were genuinely bad: programs that filled most of a child's week, aimed at making children "indistinguishable" from their peers, or taught them to hide who they are.

I take that seriously, and it is not what I do. My work is assent-based, which means your child's yes matters, however they express it, and a no is information rather than an obstacle. I don't train eye contact, I don't work against stimming, and I never set goals about making your child appear "less autistic". If something doesn't feel right for your child, we change it.

Will ABA make my child "less autistic"?

No. Being autistic is part of who your child is, not something to train away. The goals we set are about well-being: more ways to communicate, more autonomy, more play, more good days.

In my experience, what changes most is not the child but the fit between the child and the world around them: routines that work, communication that flows both ways, adults who understand what a behavior is telling them.

What's my role as a parent?

A central one. You know your child better than anyone, so goals are set with you, never over your head. And because most of your child's life happens outside our sessions, I coach you too, with concrete, compassionate tools for everyday situations, so that progress lives at home and not just in the session.

With a conversation. The introductory call is free, takes about 30 minutes, and works in English or Swedish: you tell me about your child, I tell you how I work, and you decide in your own time whether it feels right.
